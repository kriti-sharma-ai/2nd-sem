MODULE 1: Introduction to Organizational Behaviour

What is Organizational Behaviour (OB)?

Organizational Behaviour (OB) is the study of how individuals, groups, and structures influence behaviour within organizations. It focuses on understanding and managing human interactions to improve organizational effectiveness. In simple words, OB looks at how employees behave, what motivates them, and how their actions affect the company's success — understanding these patterns helps managers improve teamwork, communication and overall efficiency.

Contribution of Various Fields to OB

OB is an inter-disciplinary subject — it borrows from several behavioural and social sciences:

  • Psychology — the study of behaviour of animals and humans; seeks to measure, explain and change behaviour. Contributes ideas on personality, perception, attitude, learning, motivation, job satisfaction, training, leadership effectiveness, performance appraisal, employee selection, work design and work stress.
  • Sociology — studies group behaviour and the behaviour of people in relation to other humans. Contributes ideas on group dynamics, communication, leadership, power and politics, conflict, organisational structures, formal/informal organisations, organisational culture and organisational change.
  • Social Psychology — borrows concepts from both psychology and sociology; focuses on the influence of people on one another. Contributes ideas on behavioural change, attitude change, communication, group processes and group decision making.
  • Anthropology — the study of the human race, particularly its culture, which has a significant influence on human behaviour. Contributes ideas on individual culture, organisational culture and organisational environment.
  • Engineering — particularly industrial engineering, concerned with work measurement, productivity measurement, workflow analysis and design, and labour relations.
  • Political Science — studies the behaviour of individuals and groups within a political environment, including conflict resolution, group coalition, allocation of power and manipulation of power for self-interest.

Scope of Organizational Behaviour

OB mainly covers four aspects:

  • Individual Behaviour — personality & traits, attitudes & job satisfaction, perceptions & cognitive biases, motivation & incentives, learning & development.
  • Group Dynamics — communication patterns, leadership roles, team cohesiveness, conflict resolution, group decision-making.
  • External Environment — economic conditions, technological advancements, social trends & demographics, regulatory changes, globalization & competition.
  • Organizational Structure — hierarchy & authority, role clarity & responsibilities, types of organizational structures (flat, hierarchical, matrix), workflow & coordination, adaptability & change management.

Nature of Organizational Behaviour

  • OB is a part of general management, not the whole of management — it represents the behavioural approach to management.
  • OB contains a body of research and application associated with a growing concern for people.
  • OB is a human tool for human benefit — it helps predict behaviour of people.
  • OB is an inter-disciplinary field of study, synthesizing knowledge from Psychology, Sociology, Anthropology, Political Science, Economics, etc.
  • OB involves three levels of analysis: individual behaviour, group behaviour, and behaviour of the organization itself.
  • OB is action-oriented and goal-directed — it provides rational thinking about people and their behaviour.
  • OB is both a science and an art.
  • OB seeks to fulfil both employees' needs and organizational objectives.

Importance of Studying OB

  • Builds better relationships by achieving people, organizational, and social objectives.
  • Covers a wide array of human resource areas — behaviour, training and development, change management, leadership, teams.
  • Brings coordination, the essence of management.
  • Improves goodwill of the organization and helps achieve objectives quickly.
  • Makes optimum utilization of resources and facilitates motivation.
  • Leads to higher efficiency and improves relations in the organization.
  • Is multidisciplinary — applies different techniques, methods and theories to evaluate performance.

MODULE 2: Personality, Perception and Attitude

What Are Organizations?

Organizations are groups of people who work interdependently towards some purpose. They are not physical structures; rather, they are people who work together to achieve shared goals through structured patterns of interaction. They are usually created by an individual or group who believe they possess the skills and knowledge needed to produce goods and services.

Definition of Personality

Personality has been defined by Salvatore Maddi as: "Personality is a stable set of characteristics and tendencies that determine those commonalities and differences in the psychological behaviour (thoughts, feelings and actions) of people that have continuity in time."

Theories of Personality

1. Psychoanalytical Theory — Sigmund Freud (Father of Psychoanalysis)

Based on the Freudian concept of the unconscious nature of personality. Freud held that human behaviour and motivation are the outcome of conflicts between the Id, the Ego and the Super-ego.

  • Id (Pleasure Principle) — the foundation of the unconscious; base of libido drives; strives for immediate pleasure, with animalistic instincts of aggression, power and domination. Example: You see a cake and want to eat it immediately.
  • Ego (Reality Principle) — conscious in nature; relates our urges to the real world and keeps the Id in check so pleasures are granted at an appropriate time. Example: You wait until lunch time to eat the cake.
  • Super-ego (Moral Principle) — the higher-level restraining force, the conscience of the person, formed by absorbing cultural and ethical values. Example: You decide not to eat the cake because it belongs to someone else.

Easy Example: You find ₹100 on the floor — Id says "Keep the money!", Ego says "Let's see whose money it is", and Super-ego says "Return it to the owner, it's the right thing to do." A balance among all three forces is needed to create a "normal" personality.

Exam Answer (2 Marks): Sigmund Freud's Psychoanalytical Theory states that personality consists of three elements: Id, Ego, and Superego. Id seeks pleasure, Ego deals with reality, and Superego represents morality and conscience.

2. Trait Theory

Visualizes personality as a reflection of certain traits of the individual — some traits are common to most people, others are unique. People can be described as aggressive, loyal, pleasant, flexible, humorous, sentimental, impulsive, cool, and so on. Traits are the basic elements of personality and summarize behaviour patterns (e.g., a person behaving in an extrovert, forceful manner in most situations is labelled "aggressive"; someone who lets others take the initiative is labelled "submissive").

3. Self-Concept Theory — Carl Rogers

Organized around the idea that personality and behaviour are largely determined by the individual himself — we act in ways consistent with our own self-image. According to Rogers, behaviour is best understood through the internal frame of reference an individual has for his own self; the individual is the centre of experience, and his self-image is integral to how he views himself and perceives others' views of him.

4. Social Learning Theory of Personality

Personality is the cumulative total of all the learning of a person. It differs from psychoanalytical theory in two ways: personality development is a result of social variables (not biological drives), and motives are traced to known/conscious needs (not unconscious desires). It uses the "reinforcement and punishment" approach — e.g., frustration from the environment can bring out aggression, while good behaviour rewarded with appreciation reinforces further good behaviour.

Introduction to Perception

Perception is the psychological process that allows an individual to adjust his behaviour. It may be defined as "a cognitive process by which people attend to incoming stimuli, organise and interpret such stimuli into behaviour," or "a process by which individuals organise and interpret their sensory impressions to give meaning to their environment."

Components of Perception

Perception is a process of the sensory organs (eyes, ears, nose, tongue, skin). It has 7 components:

Meaning, Nature and Characteristics of Attitude

Attitude reflects one's feelings towards a particular object, event or person and shows a persistent tendency to act in a particular manner towards that object. Attitude consists of beliefs, feelings and action tendencies, and is often multidimensional and complex.

Attitudes have four basic characteristics: direction (favourable/unfavourable/neutral), intensity (strength of the feeling), salience (perceived importance), and differentiation (how strong the supporting beliefs/values are).

Components of Attitude

  • Evaluative Component — how we feel about something; our liking or disliking.
  • Cognitive Component — our beliefs and knowledge about the object/person/situation.
  • Behavioural Component — our predisposition to act in a certain way, based on the beliefs and feelings above.

Attitude Formation

While attitudes are basically learned over the years, some inherited characteristics affect them. Five factors shape attitude formation: Experiences, Perceptual Biases, Observation of the Other Person, Association, and Personality.

Functions of Attitude — Daniel Katz

The American psychologist Daniel Katz outlined four functions of attitudes:

  • Adjustment Function — helps individuals adjust to their environment.
  • Ego-Defensive Function — holding attitudes that protect self-esteem or justify guilt-inducing actions.
  • Value-Expressive Function — enables expression of a person's centrally held values.
  • Knowledge Function — gives a consistent, stable sense of control by predicting what is likely to happen.

Measurement of Attitude

  • Thurston(e) Attitude Scale — a questionnaire-based scaling method, widely used before computers; less dominant today.
  • Likert's Scale — every statement has five/seven response options (strongly agree → strongly disagree); scores are assigned and totalled for an overall score.
  • Interviews — obtaining information about employees' attitudes through structured interviews, ideally by representatives from outside the organization.

Work-Related Attitudes

  • Job satisfaction — a general attitude of liking or disliking one's job.
  • Job involvement — degree to which a person identifies psychologically with their job; related to psychological empowerment (belief in having influence, capability, autonomy and meaningfulness at work).
  • Organisational commitment — identification with the organisation and its goals, with a positive relationship to productivity.
  • Perceived organizational support — degree to which employees feel the organization values them and cares for their well-being.
  • Employee engagement — an individual's level of involvement, satisfaction and interest in their job.

Importance of Learning Theories

Learning theories matter because they: inform instruction (adapt teaching to learner needs), improve learning outcomes, facilitate professional development, guide research, and promote innovation in teaching methods.

Theories of Learning

  • 1. Cognitive Learning — emphasizes the role of cognitive processes (active engagement, problem-solving, mindful thinking) in understanding, retaining and applying knowledge.
  • 2. Social Learning Theory — Albert Bandura — people learn new skills by seeing, copying and imitating others' conduct in social situations; shaped by behavioural outcomes, personal characteristics and external factors.
  • 3. Operant Conditioning — B. F. Skinner — behaviour is shaped by its outcomes (reinforcement or punishment); used in therapy, parenting and education to encourage desired actions.
  • 4. Classical Conditioning — Ivan Pavlov — learning by associating stimuli, demonstrated by teaching dogs to associate food with the ring of a bell; explains phobias and other behavioural phenomena.
  • 5. Behaviorism — B. F. Skinner and John B. Watson — focuses on measurable, observable behaviours rather than internal states; behaviour changes in response to consequences from interacting with the environment.

MODULE 3 & 4: Groups, Power and Political Behaviour

What is a Group?

A group is defined as "two or more interacting and interdependent individuals who have come together to achieve particular objectives." Since an individual cannot perform all required activities alone, group formation is inevitable for achieving organizational goals. Groups may be formal (recognised) or informal (not recognised but functioning). Group dynamics is used to increase productivity and profitability — group behaviour differs from individual behaviour.

Reasons for Group Formation

  • Task Accomplishment — achieving objectives through task performance.
  • Problem Solving — uniting to solve foreseen or faced problems.
  • Proximity — based on "propinquity": people affiliate because of spatial/geographical closeness.
  • Socio-psychological Factors — sentiments and action-uniformities; groups also formed for safety, security and social achievement.

Stages of Group Development

1. Forming
Group decides purpose, structure, leadership; membership grows; communication pattern develops
↓
2. Storming
Members express views forcibly; interpersonal conflicts; authorities criticised; group gains synergy if smoothened
↓
3. Norming
Close relationships, cohesiveness, strong group identity and camaraderie develop
↓
4. Performing
Group is fully functional; works towards and achieves its goals
↓
5. Adjourning
Group dissolves, ideally after completing the task; premature adjournment is undesirable

Types of Groups

  • Formal Groups — set up by the organization for its goals: Command groups (a manager and reporting employees, defined by the org. hierarchy) and Task groups (employees working together on a particular task/project, which can cross command relationships).
  • Informal Groups — evolve naturally to meet social/affiliation needs: Friendship groups (common characteristics), Interest groups (a shared specific objective), Reference groups (used as a basis for comparison in decisions/opinions), Membership groups (any group a person actually belongs to).

Power

According to John K. Galbraith, power is "the possibility of imposing one's will on the behavior of others." The essence of power is control over the behaviour of others, and a reflection of influence one person has over others.

Power in an organizational setting comes from two sources: Interpersonal sources and Organizationally-based structural sources.

Interpersonal Sources of Power — John R. P. French & Bertram Raven

  • Legitimate power — vested in leadership to take certain actions.
  • Reward power — control over rewards (extrinsic/intrinsic) valued by another; the greater the perceived value, the greater the power.
  • Coercive power — the ability to influence through punishment.
  • Expert power — ability to control behaviour through possession of needed knowledge/expertise.
  • Referent power — also called charismatic power; based on personal attraction rather than position, so followers feel inclined to the leader.

Structural and Situational Sources of Power

  • Knowledge as power — individuals/groups with knowledge critical to organizational goals hold power.
  • Resources as power — anyone who can provide resources (capital, personnel, equipment, raw materials, customers) critical to the organization acquires power.
  • Decision making as power — authority to decide, or the ability to influence decision-makers, is a source of power; real power may rest with those who strongly influence the final decision-maker rather than the decision-maker alone.

Power Tactics

Power tactics are the manner in which authority figures exercise their ability to control or influence others, often to ensure compliance or enact behaviour/attitude change. There are nine commonly identified power tactics:

Meaning of Politics — J. Pfeffer

Politics has been defined by J. Pfeffer as "those activities taken within organizations to acquire, develop and use power and other resources to obtain one's preferred outcomes in a situation in which there is uncertainty or dissensus about choices." Political behaviour is typically self-serving, aimed at enhancing or protecting one's own interests or the interests of one's group.

Types of Political Activities

  • Networking — building relationships with influential people to gain support/resources.
  • Coalition Building — forming alliances to combine power and influence.
  • Lobbying — actively influencing decision-makers to support a position or policy.
  • Controlling Information — selectively sharing/withholding information to shape perceptions.
  • Favoritism and Patronage — providing favours/rewards to gain support and loyalty.
  • Impression Management — behaving to create a favourable impression, particularly with those in power.

Factors Contributing to Organizational Politics

  • Resource Scarcity — limited budgets, promotions, opportunities lead to competition.
  • Ambiguity in Roles and Policies — unclear roles/responsibilities create room for manoeuvring.
  • Diverse Interests and Goals — differing individual/group goals drive political activity.
  • Organizational Change — mergers/restructuring increase political behaviour as people protect their position.

Positive and Negative Impacts of Organizational Politics

Positive: drives innovation and change, helps resolve conflict (negotiation/compromise), and can aid career advancement through alliances and visibility.

Negative: reduces morale (perceived unfairness/favoritism), decreases trust, causes inefficiency (time/energy diverted), and increases employee turnover.

Managing Organizational Politics

  • Promote Transparency — clear communication and decision-making reduce the need for political behaviour.
  • Establish Clear Policies — well-defined roles/responsibilities minimise ambiguity.
  • Foster a Positive Culture — collaboration, fairness and ethics mitigate negative politics.
  • Develop Political Skills — train employees/leaders in ethical influence.

Ethical considerations while engaging in political activity: Fairness, Respect, Transparency, and Accountability.

MODULE 5: International Organizational Behaviour (IOB)

Definition of IOB

International Organizational Behavior (IOB) is the study of how individuals and groups act within organizations that operate across national boundaries. It encompasses understanding, predicting and managing behaviour in multicultural and multinational contexts, integrating sociology, psychology, anthropology and management. Management and leadership styles differ significantly between, say, European, Japanese, Chinese and U.S. organizations — understanding cultural differences is essential for global business.

Dimensions of Culture

  • Self Image — in some countries people are treated as honest and trustworthy; in others, with suspicion. People carry their home-country values with them when travelling.
  • Relationship with World — some societies try to dominate their surroundings (e.g., USA, Canada); others build harmony with their environment (e.g., many Asian countries, who plant crops according to environmental conditions).
  • Individualism vs Collectivism — the USA, Great Britain and Canada have cultures that encourage individualism; Japan, China and Israel promote collectivism (group harmony, unity, loyalty). This affects hiring: individualist cultures evaluate applicants on personal/educational/professional achievement; collectivist cultures evaluate on trustworthiness, loyalty and compatibility with coworkers.
  • Time Dimension — the USA/Canada focus on the present and near future; Europeans give more importance to the past; Japanese firms have a long-term future orientation and often retain employees for a lifetime.
  • Public and Private Space — North America favours private space (a more restricted/confined manager is seen as more important; visitors go through a secretary first); Japan is more public-oriented (bosses sit together with employees in the same room).

Communication Problems Across Cultures

  • Perceptual Problems — perception affects how people see and interpret the same reality differently.
  • Stereotyping Problems — assuming a person belongs to a particular class, ignoring individual differences within a culture; used to build an overall (often inaccurate) profile of others.
  • Ethnocentric Problems (Ethnocentrism) — the sense of superiority held by members of a particular culture (e.g., the belief that one's own country is "best in all areas"); promoted by all cultures to some degree, and a major source of communication problems.

Impact of Culture on Organizational Behaviour

  • Communication Styles — language barriers cause misunderstandings; high-context cultures (Japan, China) rely on implicit messages and context; low-context cultures (USA, Germany) communicate explicitly and directly.
  • Leadership and Management Styles — hierarchical cultures (India, Mexico) favour autocratic, top-down decisions; egalitarian cultures (Sweden, Netherlands) favour participative, collaborative decision-making.
  • Motivation and Incentives — individualistic cultures (USA, UK) emphasize personal achievement and individual rewards; collectivist cultures (Japan, Korea) emphasize group achievement and team-based rewards.
  • Decision-Making Processes — some cultures value consensus and harmony (Japan); others prioritise individual autonomy and speed (USA). Risk approach also varies: risk-averse/detailed planning (Germany) vs. risk-tolerant (USA).
  • Conflict Resolution — cultures avoiding direct confrontation prefer mediation (China, Japan); cultures valuing directness confront issues openly (USA, Israel). Collectivist cultures may suppress conflict to preserve harmony; individualist cultures address it openly.
  • Workplace Relationships — some cultures keep strict formal hierarchies (France, India); others are informal/egalitarian (Australia, Canada). In some cultures personal relationships are integral to business (Latin America, Middle East); in others, personal and professional lives are kept separate (USA, Germany).

Motivational Differences Across Cultures

  • Religion — religious values directly and indirectly affect motivational levels, working style, and ultimately economic growth.
  • Uncertainty — employees comfortable with ambiguity switch jobs more freely and are unconcerned about job security; those with high uncertainty avoidance prefer to specialize, avoid conflict, and interact with others to understand the company's situation.
  • Power Distance — people in the U.S., Britain and Canada have more trouble accepting that others hold more power than them, so empowerment programmes are more successful there.

Current Trends in OB

  • Remote and Hybrid Work — accelerated by COVID-19; affects performance management, team cohesion, employee engagement; reliance on virtual collaboration tools.
  • Employee Well-being and Mental Health — wellness programs, mental health resources, promoting work-life balance to reduce burnout.
  • Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI) — DEI initiatives, bias training, fostering inclusive cultures to attract/retain talent and drive innovation.
  • Technology and Automation — AI, machine learning and automation enhance efficiency but require continuous learning and upskilling.
  • Agile and Adaptive Organizations — flexible, responsive structures; breaking down hierarchies; cross-functional teams.
  • Sustainability and Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) — integrating sustainable practices, aligning with societal expectations.
  • Employee Experience and Engagement — leveraging feedback, technology and personalization to improve satisfaction, motivation and retention.
